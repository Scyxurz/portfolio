Halachos of lighting an electric menorah on Chanukah

Chanukah, also commonly spelled Hanukkah, is an 8 day long Jewish holiday celebrating the recovery of the temple in Jerusalem around 200 BCE, having been conquered by the Greeks about a century prior. Many Jews still celebrate this holiday today by lighting candles on a menorah, or hanukkiah, which is a candelabra with 9 arms. The shamesh, the candle used to light the others, is lit each day, plus one additional candle per day of the holiday up to 8 on the last day.

However, there are some arguments as to what constitutes lighting a menorah. For the halachos (laws) of Shabbat, it is said that turning on an incandescent light is similar to lighting a fire since the filament gets hot enough to burn, and lighting fire on Shabbat is forbidden because it was a form of work done in the mishkan (tabernacle) back in the times of the temple. Shabbat is supposed to be a day of rest, so no work is allowed to be done that day.

This drasha (sermon) by Rabbi Katz from congregation Anshe Chesed in Linden, New Jersey goes over the topic of using lightbulbs in an electric menorah, and if that fulfills the requirement of actually lighting a menorah for Chanukah.
